Airspace.

CLASS A, AIM 3-2-2. Controlled airspace from 18,000 feet MSL to Flight Level 600, within the 48 contiguous states and Alaska. Includes the airspace within 12 nautical miles of the shoreline, as well as designated international airspace beyond that 12 nautical mile distance. IFR only, unless otherwise authorized.

CLASS B, AIM 3-2-3, Section 91.131. Controlled airspace surrounding the nation's busiest airports. Usually extends from the surface up to 10,000 feet MSL. The shape of each Class B is specifically tailored to its environment, consisting of a surface area and two or more layers, resembling an upside-down wedding cake. ATC separates both VFR and IFR traffic. Requires ATC clearance to enter — VFR pilots must make sure they hear a clearance to "enter Class B"; IFR pilots will typically already have this clearance as part of their ATC clearance, picked up before or after takeoff. A Mode-C transponder and ADS-B Out equipment are required within a 30 nautical mile radius, called the "Mode-C Veil."

CLASS C, AIM 3-2-4. Controlled airspace around towered airports with a certain number of IFR operations or passenger volume. Typical inner area is a 5 nautical mile radius surrounding the primary airport, extending up to 4,000 feet above airport height. A 10 nautical mile radius shelf area typically extends from no lower than 1,200 feet up to 4,000 feet above airport height. A non-charted outer area extends up to 20 nautical miles from the primary airport, where ATC provides VFR and IFR traffic separation if two-way radio communication is established, and within the Class C airspace itself. Requires two-way radio communication, a Mode-C transponder, and ADS-B Out equipment.

CLASS D, AIM 3-2-5. Controlled airspace extending from the surface to 2,500 feet above airport height, usually shaped as a cylinder with a 4 nautical mile radius from the primary airport. Requires two-way radio communication.

CLASS E, AIM 3-2-6. Controlled airspace not designated as A, B, C, or D. May or may not be associated with an airport. Requires Mode-C transponder and ADS-B Out equipment at and above 10,000 feet MSL within the 48 contiguous states and DC, excluding at or below 2,500 feet AGL. Requires ADS-B Out at and above 3,000 feet MSL over the Gulf of Mexico, from the US coast out to 12 nautical miles. Functions of Class E include: surface area designated for an airport; extension to a surface area of Class B, C, or D; transition area, beginning at 700 or 1,200 feet AGL, used to transition to or from a terminal or en-route environment; en-route domestic areas; federal airways and low altitude RNAV routes; and offshore airspace areas.

CLASS G, AIM 3-3. Uncontrolled airspace. Class G is generally any airspace that has not been designated as Class A, B, C, D, or E.

BASIC VFR WEATHER MINIMUMS, Section 91.155. Minimum visibility and distance-from-clouds mnemonics: "3152" means 3 statute miles visibility, 1,000 feet above clouds, 500 feet below, 2,000 feet horizontal. "1152" means 1 statute mile visibility, 1,000 feet above, 500 feet below, 2,000 feet horizontal. "5111" means 5 statute miles visibility, 1,000 feet above, 1,000 feet below, 1 statute mile horizontal.

By airspace: Class A is IFR only. Class B, C, and D require 3 statute miles visibility and remaining clear of clouds. Class E and Class G at or above 10,000 feet MSL require "5111." Class E below 10,000 feet MSL requires "3152." Class G above 1,200 feet AGL but below 10,000 feet MSL requires "1152" by day, "3152" by night. Class G at or below 1,200 feet AGL requires, by day, 1 statute mile and clear of clouds; by night, "3152," or 1 statute mile clear of clouds if in a traffic pattern within half a statute mile of a runway.

When the cloud ceiling is less than 1,000 feet, no person may operate an aircraft beneath the ceiling under VFR within the lateral boundaries of controlled airspace designated to the surface for an airport, except under Special VFR, Section 91.157. Minimum ground visibility for landing, takeoff, or entering a traffic pattern at an airport within the lateral boundaries of Class B, C, D, or E surface areas is 3 statute miles; if ground visibility is not reported, use flight visibility, except under Special VFR. For the purpose of Section 91.155, an aircraft operating at the base altitude of a Class E airspace area is considered to be within the airspace directly below that area.

SPECIAL USE AIRSPACE.

Prohibited Areas, Section 91.133, AIM 3-4-2. Flight is prohibited unless permission is granted by the using or controlling agency. Prohibited airspace exists due to security or other reasons associated with the national welfare. Example: Prohibited airspace P-56A over the White House.

Restricted Areas, Section 91.133, AIM 3-4-3. Flight is not completely prohibited, but is subject to restrictions due to hazards to aircraft such as artillery firing, aerial gunnery, or guided missiles. No person may operate an aircraft within a restricted area contrary to the restrictions imposed, unless that person has permission of the using or controlling agency. If the restricted airspace is not active and has been released to the FAA, ATC will allow the aircraft to operate in it without a specific clearance. If it is active and has not been released, ATC will issue a clearance ensuring the aircraft avoids the restricted airspace, unless it is on an approved altitude reservation mission or has obtained its own permission and informs the controlling agency.

Warning Areas, AIM 3-4-4. Extend 3 nautical miles outward from the coast of the US. Contain activity that may be hazardous to aircraft. The purpose of warning areas is to warn nonparticipating aircraft of the potential hazard. May be located over domestic or international water, or both.

Military Operating Areas, or MOAs, AIM 3-4-5. Established to separate certain military training activities from IFR traffic. When a MOA is in use, nonparticipating IFR aircraft may be cleared through it if IFR separation can be provided; otherwise, ATC will reroute or restrict the traffic. Example activities: air combat tactics, air intercepts, aerobatics, formation training, and low-altitude tactics. VFR pilots should exercise extreme caution operating within an active MOA, and should contact any FSS within 100 miles of the area for accurate real-time hours of operation, and contact the controlling agency for traffic advisories prior to entering.

Alert Areas, AIM 3-4-6. Depicted on charts to inform pilots of a high volume of pilot training or an unusual type of aerial activity. Pilots transitioning the area are equally responsible for collision avoidance.

Controlled Firing Areas, AIM 3-4-7. Contain activities that, if not conducted in a controlled environment, may be hazardous to nonparticipating aircraft. Activities are suspended immediately when a spotter aircraft, radar, or ground lookout positions indicate an aircraft might be approaching the area. Controlled Firing Areas are not charted, because they do not cause a nonparticipating aircraft to change its flight path.

Military Training Routes, or MTRs, AIM 3-5-2. IFR MTRs, designated "IR," are typically above 1,500 feet AGL; VFR MTRs, designated "VR," are below 1,500 feet AGL. Generally established below 10,000 feet at speeds in excess of 250 knots, though route segments may exist at higher altitudes. Route identification: MTRs with no segments above 1,500 feet AGL are identified by 4 digits, for example IR1206 or VR1207; MTRs with one or more segments above 1,500 feet AGL are identified by 3 digits, for example IR206 or VR207.

Air Defense Identification Zone, or ADIZ, AIM 5-6. An area of airspace over land or water in which the ready identification, location, and control of all aircraft, except DOD and law enforcement aircraft, is required in the interest of national security. Requirements to operate within an ADIZ: an operable transponder with altitude encoding, two-way radio communication with the appropriate aeronautical facility, and filing an IFR or Defense VFR, or DVFR, flight plan. Depart within 5 minutes of the flight plan's estimated departure time.

Temporary Flight Restrictions, or TFRs, AIM 3-5-3. Defined in Flight Data Center, or FDC, NOTAMs. TFR NOTAMs begin with the phrase "FLIGHT RESTRICTIONS." Current TFRs are found at tfr.faa.gov. Some reasons the FAA may establish a TFR: to protect persons or property in the air or on the surface from hazards by low-flying aircraft; to provide a safe environment for disaster relief aircraft; to prevent unsafe congestion of sightseeing aircraft around an event of high public interest; to protect declared national disasters for humanitarian reasons in the State of Hawaii; to protect the President, Vice President, or other public figures; and to provide a safe environment for space agency operations.

Special Flight Rules Area, or SFRA, AIM 3-5-7. An airspace of defined dimensions above land areas or territorial waters where special air traffic rules have been established. Each person operating in a Special Air Traffic Rules area or SFRA must adhere to the special air traffic rules in 14 CFR Part 93, unless otherwise authorized or required by ATC. Example: the Washington DC Metropolitan SFRA.

MAX AIRCRAFT AIRSPEEDS IN THE US, Section 91.117. Mach 1.0, the speed of sound, above 10,000 feet MSL, Section 91.817. 250 knots below 10,000 feet MSL. 200 knots under Class B, or within a VFR corridor through Class B. 200 knots at or below 2,500 feet within 4 nautical miles of the primary airport of a Class C or D airspace. If the aircraft's minimum safe airspeed for a particular operation is greater than the max speed prescribed above, the aircraft may be operated at that minimum speed.
